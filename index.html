What is this?

"Are you straight or gay?"
"Hold on, let me get my data."

The bi-cycle is a phenomenon widely discussed in bisexual and queer communities: the way attraction to people of different genders tends to shift and fluctuate over time. For many bisexuals, these fluctuations can fuel insecurity — doubt about whether they are "really bisexual", anxiety in relationships, or the feeling that their sexuality doesn't match expectations on any given day.

Despite being a common topic in queer spaces, the bi-cycle has received little formal academic attention. This project is an attempt to change that — or at least to kickstart the conversation about how it could be studied rigorously.

What you'll find here: a daily diary dataset collected over ~3 years, R scripts and a Shiny app for structural equation modelling, a browser-based tracker others can use to collect their own data, and an essay documenting the whole thing.

This is an independent, personal project. The author is not a psychologist or social scientist — they are a chemist who fell down a statistical rabbit hole and found it unexpectedly affirming. All tools used are free and open-source, and everything is shared here so that others can replicate, critique, and maybe add to it.

The Project

How a Reddit meme became a three-year dataset.

How it started

It started, as many things do, with memes on Reddit. After finding r/bisexual and recognising the bi-cycle in its discussions, tracking seemed like a funny idea. Over time, patterns seemed to emerge. Those patterns became hypotheses; those hypotheses became this project.

The data

Data was collected via a daily diary method over approximately three years (2023–2025), yielding ~1,096 observations. Each day, the following was recorded:

Context

Day of week and activity context: work, gym, social, education, inactive, or other.

Attraction indicators

Attraction, fantasy, lust, romantic interest, and sexual activity — each recorded daily as binary or ordinal indicators.

Gender categories

Directed toward men (M), androgynous people (X), and women (F). This structure supports polychoric correlations and WLSMV estimation.

The latent variable problem

Attraction is unobservable. It is, in the language of statistics, a latent variable — something real and consequential, but not directly measurable. You can only get at it through the things it produces: feelings, fantasies, the way a stranger across a café might catch your eye. This makes it a natural candidate for structural equation modelling (SEM), a family of multivariate techniques designed precisely for situations where the variables you care about most are the ones you can't directly observe.

Methods

SEM, MIMIC models, and a small simulation study.

Structural equation modelling

The core analysis uses the lavaan package in R. The measurement model specifies three latent variables — Attraction, Fantasy, and Lust — each indicated by gender-specific observed items. The structural model then examines how daily context predicts these latent constructs, and how the constructs relate to each other.

Estimator: WLSMV (diagonally weighted least squares with mean and variance adjustment) — appropriate for binary and ordinal indicators. Model fit assessed using CFI, TLI, RMSEA, and SRMR.

MIMIC model

A Multiple Indicators, Multiple Causes (MIMIC) framework is used to examine how background variables — activity context, day of week, and others — influence the latent factors while accounting for measurement error. The Shiny app includes five built-in predictors and allows users to specify their own.

Simulation study

Because SEM estimation can be challenging with small samples, a simulation study was conducted to determine the minimum number of days that need to be tracked before the model can be estimated reliably. This informs guidance given to others who want to use the tracker and analyse their own data.

Tools

Everything needed to replicate, explore, or run your own version.

Tracker

A self-contained HTML file. Open in any modern browser — no installation or internet connection required. Export your data as CSV.

Shiny app

Interactive R app with descriptive stats, MIMIC modelling, full SEM output, a calendar heatmap, and CSV downloads. Ships with embedded example data.

R scripts

Standalone scripts for the analysis pipeline. Loads lavaan, casts variables as ordered factors, fits the WLSMV model, and exports results to CSV.

Getting started

Download and install R and RStudio (both free). Download the repository and extract it to a location of your choice. Run the Prerequisites script to install the required packages (shiny, lavaan, DT, shinythemes, semPlot), then open any script or the Shiny app from RStudio.

Using the tracker

Open bicycle_tracker_final.html in any modern browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, or Edge). No installation or internet connection is required. Your data is stored locally in your browser until you export it as a CSV file, which can then be used directly with the R scripts and Shiny app.

Shiny app features

The app includes stacked proportional bar charts of attraction by gender displayed in bi flag colours, the full MIMIC model interface with customisable predictors, structural estimates and fit measures, a day-by-day calendar heatmap of gender-bias scores, and CSV downloads of all parameter estimates and model coefficients. It can be explored immediately using the embedded example data.

Notes & Acknowledgements

A few things worth keeping in mind.

  • Data collected by one person is specific to that person. It is not possible to draw conclusions from this data about bisexuals in general.
  • The analyses are exploratory and proof-of-concept in nature. No claims are made about bisexuals as a population.
  • Self-report limitations apply. Daily diary data is subject to recall bias, mood effects, and the general messiness of asking someone to quantify something as slippery as attraction.
  • The measurement model is theory-driven but not validated on an independent sample. The simulation study addresses robustness; external validation awaits future research.
  • Gender categories. The three categories used (M, X, F) are a practical simplification. Gender is more complex than this, and nobody owes you a specific pattern of attraction or gender presentation.
