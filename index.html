What is this?

"Are you straight or gay?"
"Hold on, let me get my data."

The bi-cycle is a phenomenon widely discussed in bisexual and queer communities: the way attraction to people of different genders tends to shift and fluctuate over time. For many bisexuals, these fluctuations can fuel insecurity — doubt about whether they are "really bisexual", anxiety in relationships, or the feeling that their sexuality doesn't match expectations on any given day.

Despite being a common topic in queer spaces, the bi-cycle has received little formal academic attention. This project is an attempt to change that — or at least to kickstart the conversation about how it could be studied rigorously.

What you'll find here: a daily diary dataset collected over ~3 years, R scripts and a Shiny app for structural equation modelling, a browser-based tracker others can use to collect their own data, and an essay documenting the whole thing.

This is an independent, personal project. The author is not a psychologist or social scientist — they are a chemist who fell down a statistical rabbit hole and found it unexpectedly affirming. All tools used are free and open-source, and everything is shared here so that others can replicate, critique, and maybe add to it.

The Project

How a Reddit meme became a three-year dataset.

How it started

It started, as many things do, with memes on Reddit. After finding r/bisexual and recognising the bi-cycle in its discussions, tracking seemed like a funny idea. Over time, patterns seemed to emerge. Those patterns became hypotheses; those hypotheses became this project.

The data

Data was collected via a daily diary method over approximately three years (2023–2025), yielding ~1,096 observations. Each day, the following was recorded:

Context

Day of week and activity context: work, gym, social, education, inactive, or other.

Attraction indicators

Attraction, fantasy, lust, romantic interest, and sexual activity — each recorded daily as binary or ordinal indicators.

Gender categories

Directed toward men (M), androgynous people (X), and women (F). This structure supports polychoric correlations and WLSMV estimation.

The latent variable problem

Attraction is unobservable. It is, in the language of statistics, a latent variable — something real and consequential, but not directly measurable. You can only get at it through the things it produces: feelings, fantasies, the way a stranger across a café might catch your eye. This makes it a natural candidate for structural equation modelling (SEM), a family of multivariate techniques designed precisely for situations where the variables you care about most are the ones you can't directly observe.

Methods

SEM, MIMIC models, and a small simulation study.

Structural equation modelling

The core analysis uses the lavaan package in R. The measurement model specifies three latent variables — Attraction, Fantasy, and Lust — each indicated by gender-specific observed items. The structural model then examines how daily context predicts these latent constructs, and how the constructs relate to each other.

Estimator: WLSMV (diagonally weighted least squares with mean and variance adjustment) — appropriate for binary and ordinal indicators. Model fit assessed using CFI, TLI, RMSEA, and SRMR.

MIMIC model

A Multiple Indicators, Multiple Causes (MIMIC) framework is used to examine how background variables — activity context, day of week, and others — influence the latent factors while accounting for measurement error. The Shiny app includes five built-in predictors and allows users to specify their own.

Simulation study

Because SEM estimation can be challenging with small samples, a simulation study was conducted to determine the minimum number of days that need to be tracked before the model can be estimated reliably. This informs guidance given to others who want to use the tracker and analyse their own data.

Tools

Everything needed to replicate, explore, or run your own version.

Tracker

A self-contained HTML file. Open in any modern browser — no installation or internet connection required. Export your data as CSV.

Shiny app

Interactive R app with descriptive stats, MIMIC modelling, full SEM output, a calendar heatmap, and CSV downloads. Ships with embedded example data.

R scripts

Standalone scripts for the analysis pipeline. Loads lavaan, casts variables as ordered factors, fits the WLSMV model, and exports results to CSV.

Getting started

Download and install R and RStudio (both free). Download the repository and extract it to a location of your choice. Run the Prerequisites script to install the required packages (shiny, lavaan, DT, shinythemes, semPlot), then open any script or the Shiny app from RStudio.

Using the tracker

Open bicycle_tracker_final.html in any modern browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, or Edge). No installation or internet connection is required. Your data is stored locally in your browser until you export it as a CSV file, which can then be used directly with the R scripts and Shiny app.

Shiny app features

The app includes stacked proportional bar charts of attraction by gender displayed in bi flag colours, the full MIMIC model interface with customisable predictors, structural estimates and fit measures, a day-by-day calendar heatmap of gender-bias scores, and CSV downloads of all parameter estimates and model coefficients. It can be explored immediately using the embedded example data.

Notes & Acknowledgements

A few things worth keeping in mind.

  • Data collected by one person is specific to that person. It is not possible to draw conclusions from this data about bisexuals in general.
  • The analyses are exploratory and proof-of-concept in nature. No claims are made about bisexuals as a population.
  • Self-report limitations apply. Daily diary data is subject to recall bias, mood effects, and the general messiness of asking someone to quantify something as slippery as attraction.
  • The measurement model is theory-driven but not validated on an independent sample. The simulation study addresses robustness; external validation awaits future research.
  • Gender categories. The three categories used (M, X, F) are a practical simplification. Gender is more complex than this, and nobody owes you a specific pattern of attraction or gender presentation.

Running the analysis

Non-convergence is common in SEM and usually means one of a few things. It is recommended to have at least 200 observations and no empty columns. The next things to check are: near-zero variance in one or more indicators (if you almost never recorded attraction toward a particular gender, that indicator has nothing to work with), and extreme item sparsity (very few "1"s or very few "0"s on a binary item).

Try inspecting your data with the descriptive statistics tab in the Shiny app before running the model. If an indicator of a factor or a predictor has very little to no observations, the model won't be able to run. Predictors can simply be excluded in the SEM app but indicators of factors might need a closer look.

It depends on what "bad" means and what you're using the model for. Standard cutoffs (CFI ≥ 0.95, RMSEA ≤ 0.06) were derived from continuous, multivariate-normal data and don't map cleanly onto binary or ordinal indicators estimated with WLSMV. Some degradation in fit indices is expected. The more important question is whether the model is conceptually sound and whether the parameter estimates make sense.

If fit is very poor (CFI below 0.90, RMSEA above 0.10), it may be worth checking whether the model structure is appropriate for your data — for example, whether all three latent variables are actually identifiable given your response patterns.

WLSMV doesn't produce a true likelihood, so AIC and BIC — which are likelihood-based — are not valid under this estimator. lavaan will either refuse to compute them or produce meaningless values. For model comparison under WLSMV you can use the scaled difference chi-square test (for nested models) or compare fit indices like CFI and RMSEA directly. If you really need information criteria, you can temporarily refit the model using MLR, which does produce a likelihood — but note that MLR makes stronger distributional assumptions that may not hold for binary/ordinal data.

The simulation study in the repository was run specifically to answer this question. Check the results there for a concrete minimum N. As a rough heuristic: more is always better, and the model is unlikely to converge reliably with fewer than a few hundred observations given the number of parameters being estimated.

The tracker

No. The tracker runs entirely in your browser and stores data locally using your browser's built-in storage. Nothing is sent to any server. When you export your data as CSV, that file lives on your own device. Clearing your browser data will delete your tracker history, so export regularly if you're collecting over a long period.

Missing data is handled in the analysis — lavaan with WLSMV uses pairwise present data by default. The occasional missed day is fine. If you have long gaps or missing data that isn't random (e.g. you only tracked on days when something notable happened), that's more of a concern and worth keeping in mind when interpreting results.

Glossary

Key terms used throughout the project. Click any term to expand its definition.

Measurement & model structure

A variable that cannot be directly observed or measured. It is inferred from a set of observed indicators that it is assumed to cause. In this project, attraction, fantasy, and lust are latent variables — real phenomena that can only be accessed through the things they produce, such as recorded feelings or behaviours.

A variable that is directly measured and recorded. In this project, each daily diary item — e.g. whether attraction toward men was recorded as present or absent — is an observed indicator. Multiple indicators are combined in the measurement model to estimate the underlying latent variable.

A family of multivariate statistical techniques that combine a measurement model (relating observed indicators to latent variables) with a structural model (specifying relationships between latent variables and/or predictors). SEM allows you to model complex, multi-variable systems while accounting for measurement error — something simpler approaches like regression cannot do.

A type of SEM used to test whether a hypothesised factor structure fits the observed data. Unlike exploratory factor analysis, CFA specifies in advance which indicators belong to which latent factor. The measurement model in this project is a CFA: it pre-specifies that attraction, fantasy, and lust indicators each load onto their respective latent factors.

Multiple Indicators, Multiple Causes. A type of SEM model where latent variables are simultaneously regressed on external predictors (the "causes") while being measured by observed indicators (the "indicators"). In this project, the MIMIC model examines how contextual variables like day of week and activity predict the latent attraction factors.

A visual representation of an SEM model. Observed variables are shown as rectangles, latent variables as ovals, and relationships as arrows. Single-headed arrows represent directional effects (regressions or factor loadings); double-headed arrows represent covariances. Path diagrams make the structure of a model readable at a glance.

A data collection approach in which participants record information about themselves at regular intervals — typically once per day — over an extended period. This captures within-person variation over time, which cross-sectional surveys cannot. It is well-suited to studying fluctuating phenomena like mood, behaviour, or, in this case, attraction.

Estimation

Weighted Least Squares with Mean and Variance adjustment. An estimator used in SEM when indicators are binary or ordinal rather than continuous. Unlike maximum likelihood, WLSMV does not assume multivariate normality and does not produce a true likelihood — which means AIC and BIC cannot be used for model comparison under this estimator. It is the appropriate default for data like this project's.

A correlation coefficient estimated between two ordinal variables by assuming that each is a discretised version of an underlying continuous, normally distributed variable. More appropriate than Pearson correlations for ordinal or binary data. WLSMV estimation in lavaan uses a polychoric correlation matrix as its input.

Maximum Likelihood with Robust standard errors. An estimator that produces a true likelihood, making AIC and BIC valid for model comparison. However, MLR assumes approximately continuous, multivariate-normal data — an assumption that doesn't hold well for binary or ordinal indicators. It can be used temporarily to obtain information criteria, but WLSMV is more appropriate for the main analysis.

An iterative estimation algorithm is said to have converged when successive iterations no longer produce meaningful changes in parameter estimates. Non-convergence — where the algorithm fails to settle on a stable solution — usually indicates insufficient data, near-zero variance in an indicator, or a model that is misspecified or too complex for the available data.

Model fit

A fit index that compares the hypothesised model to a baseline "null" model in which all variables are uncorrelated. Values range from 0 to 1; conventionally, CFI ≥ 0.95 is considered good fit. Higher is better. Note that these cutoffs were developed for continuous data and should be interpreted cautiously for ordinal indicators estimated with WLSMV.

Similar to CFI, but penalises model complexity. Also known as the Non-Normed Fit Index (NNFI). Values above 0.95 are conventionally considered acceptable. Unlike CFI, TLI can exceed 1.0 in well-fitting, parsimonious models.

A fit index that estimates the discrepancy between the model and the population covariance matrix, per degree of freedom. Lower is better; conventionally, RMSEA ≤ 0.06 is considered good fit and ≤ 0.08 acceptable. Unlike CFI and TLI, RMSEA penalises model complexity and rewards parsimony.

The average discrepancy between the observed and model-implied correlation matrix, expressed in standardised units. Values below 0.08 are conventionally considered acceptable. Unlike RMSEA, SRMR does not penalise complexity and tends to favour models with more parameters.

The Akaike Information Criterion and Bayesian Information Criterion are likelihood-based indices used to compare competing models — lower values indicate a better balance of fit and parsimony. They are only valid when the model has been estimated using a full-information likelihood-based estimator such as MLR. They cannot be meaningfully computed under WLSMV.

Data & context

A variable that takes one of only two values — typically 0 (absent) or 1 (present). Many of the daily diary indicators in this project are binary: either a type of attraction was recorded that day, or it wasn't.

A variable whose values have a meaningful rank order, but where the distances between values are not assumed to be equal. A rating from 1 to 5 is ordinal — a 4 is more than a 3, but not necessarily twice as much. Treating ordinal variables as continuous (and applying methods that assume equal intervals) can distort results; polychoric correlations and WLSMV avoid this.

A colloquial term used in bisexual and queer communities to describe the phenomenon whereby attraction to people of different genders fluctuates over time — cycling between periods of stronger attraction toward one gender and another. It is not a clinical or formally defined construct, which is part of what makes it an interesting target for empirical measurement.

Variation in a variable across time or occasions within the same individual, as opposed to variation between different individuals. The bi-cycle is fundamentally a within-person phenomenon — the question is how a single person's attraction changes over time, not how bisexuals differ from each other. Daily diary data is designed to capture exactly this kind of variation.

A systematic distortion in self-reported data arising from imperfect memory. People tend to remember salient, recent, or emotionally significant events more accurately than mundane ones. Daily diary methods reduce recall bias relative to retrospective surveys by collecting data close to the time of the experience.

Quick setup

  1. Download and install R and RStudio — both are free.
  2. Download or clone the repository and extract it to a location of your choice.
